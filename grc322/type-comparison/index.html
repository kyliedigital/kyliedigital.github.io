Franklin Gothic vs. Trade Gothic

History of each typeface

Franklin Gothic was designed by Morris Fuller Benton, chief type designer at American Type Founders (ATF), the largest US type foundry of the era. The typeface was designed in 1902 at ATF's offices in the US, released the same year. Franklin Gothic was created to meet demand for bold, attention-grabbing sans-serif display type for advertising and headlines, standing out against serif body text. Named after Benjamin Franklin as a distinctly American headline face, with thick, warm, slightly irregular strokes rather than geometric rigidity. This typeface is part of Benton's broader "gothic" family (alongside News Gothic) that helped define American commercial type.

Trade Gothic was designed by Jackson Burke, type director at Linotype's American branch. Burke developed this typeface between 1948 and 1960 at Linotype in the US, released in stages rather than as a single family. Trade Gothic was created as a practical, no-nonsense workhorse sans-serif for trade and commercial printing, drawing on the American gothic tradition rather than Europe's geometric approaches. Released piecemeal over a decade, each weight cut somewhat independently, giving it an irregular, organic character (rather than mechanical consistency like Helvetica) — a quality that's made it a lasting favorite in editorial and newspaper design.
